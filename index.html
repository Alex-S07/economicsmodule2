01 🌍

Basic Economics Concepts

Definition Economics is the study of scarcity, choice, and resource allocation to satisfy unlimited human wants using limited resources.

🔴 Scarcity

Resources are limited but human wants are unlimited. Root cause of all economic problems.

🎯 Choice

Because of scarcity, individuals must choose between alternatives.

💡 Opportunity Cost

The value of the next best alternative forgone when a choice is made.

🏭 Factors of Production

  • Land – natural resources
  • Labour – human effort
  • Capital – man-made tools/machines
  • Entrepreneurship – risk-taking organisation

📈 Demand

Quantity willing and able to buy at various prices (ceteris paribus).

📦 Supply

Quantity producers willing and able to offer at various prices.

😊 Utility

Satisfaction obtained from consuming a good or service.

💰 Profit

Excess of Revenue over Cost. Profit = Revenue − Cost.

🔑 Types of Economics

Microeconomics

Studies individual units — consumers, firms, markets. Example: How a company prices its product.

Macroeconomics

Studies the whole economy — GDP, inflation, employment. Example: How inflation affects the country.

Positive Economics

Facts and cause-effect. Objective, testable. "Higher price → less demand."

Normative Economics

Value judgements, opinions. "Government should reduce taxes for the poor."

⚙️ Basic Economic Problems (3 Questions Every Economy Must Answer)

1. WHAT to produce?

Which goods & in what quantity? Resources are limited — more of one = less of another.

2. HOW to produce?

Labour-intensive (more workers) vs Capital-intensive (more machines)?

3. FOR WHOM to produce?

Who gets the output? Based on income, purchasing power, govt. policy.

Electricity ExampleWhat: Power for industry or households? | How: Thermal, Solar, or Wind? | For Whom: Industries, Urban, or Rural?
02 📉

Production Possibility Curve (PPC)

Definition PPC (also PPF) shows all combinations of two goods an economy can produce with given resources & technology, assuming full & efficient use.
4 Core Concepts: Scarcity • Choice • Opportunity Cost • Efficiency → "SCOE"

📌 On the PPC

Full utilisation of resources. Efficient production point.

⬆️ Above/Beyond PPC

Unattainable — beyond current productive capacity.

⬇️ Below PPC

Underutilisation of resources. Inefficient point.

📐 Shape

Downward sloping & concave. Also called: Production Boundary / Transformation Curve.

📋 Assumptions of PPC

#Assumption
1Only two goods can be produced
2Same resources used for both goods
3Quantity & quality of resources remain constant
4Technology remains constant
5Resources are fully employed
6Efficient methods of production are used

↔️ Shifts in PPC

➡️ Rightward Shift (Growth)

  • Discovery of new resources
  • Advancement in technology

⬅️ Leftward Shift (Decline)

  • Resources destroyed (earthquake, war)
  • Use of outdated technology
💡 Quick MemoryOpportunity cost = slope of PPC. Increasing OC → concave shape. Resources are NOT equally efficient for both goods.

Utility & Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility

Definition As a consumer consumes more and more units of a commodity, the marginal utility (extra satisfaction) from each successive unit keeps decreasing, other things constant.
DMU = Discovered by Hermann Heinrich Gossen in 1854. Also called Gossen's First Law.

📊 Total Utility (TU)

Total satisfaction from consuming ALL units combined.

➕ Marginal Utility (MU)

Extra satisfaction from consuming ONE more unit.
MU = ΔTU

2

3

4

04 🛒

Law of Demand

Definition When price increases, quantity demanded decreases; when price decreases, quantity demanded increases — ceteris paribus. (Inverse relationship)
Demand = Want + Willingness to Pay + Ability to Pay

📊 Determinants of Demand

1. Price (P)

P↑ → Q↓ | Exceptions: Giffen & Veblen goods

2. Income (Y)

Normal goods: Y↑ → Q↑
Inferior goods: Y↑ → Q↓

3. Tastes & Preferences

Favorable taste → Demand increases

4. Related Goods

Substitutes: Price of tea↑ → demand for coffee↑
Complements: Price of petrol↑ → demand for cars↓

5. Consumer Expectations

Expect price to rise → buy more now

6. Number of Customers

More buyers → higher demand

🚫 Exceptions to Law of Demand

ExceptionExplanationExample
Giffen GoodsInferior goods — when price rises, still bought more (can't afford better)Coarse rice for very poor
Veblen GoodsLuxury goods — higher price = more prestigeDiamonds, luxury cars
SpeculationExpect price to rise further → buy more nowGold, land, shares
NecessitiesDemand barely falls even if price risesSalt, medicines, basic food
Fear of ShortageDuring crises, buy more at higher pricesFuel, masks, essentials

🔄 Changes in Demand

Expansion ↘

Price FALLS → Movement DOWN the same demand curve. Quantity increases.

Contraction ↗

Price RISES → Movement UP the same demand curve. Quantity decreases.

Shift Right / Left

Non-price factors change → Entire curve shifts. Right = increase, Left = decrease.

Rightward shift causes: Income↑ (normal), Population↑, Substitute price↑, Complementary price↓, Better taste, Advertisement↑, Future price expected to rise
Leftward shift causes: Income↓, Population↓, Substitute price↓, Complementary price↑, Taste against product, Future price expected to fall
05 🏭

Law of Supply

Definition Other things constant, quantity supplied increases with price rise and decreases with price fall. (Direct relationship — upward sloping supply curve)

📈 Reasons for Law of Supply

  • Profit motive – higher price = more profit
  • Entry of new firms attracted by profit
  • Efficient use of resources at higher prices

📋 Factors Affecting Supply

  • Price of commodity
  • Cost of production
  • Number of firms
  • Technology
  • Government policy (tax/subsidy)
  • Goals of the firm

🔄 Changes in Supply

Expansion ↗

Price↑ → Move UP supply curve. More supplied.

Contraction ↘

Price↓ → Move DOWN supply curve. Less supplied.

Shift Right / Left

Non-price factors change → curve shifts. Right = increase, Left = decrease.

Rightward Shift: Cost↓, Technology improved, Subsidies, More firms
Leftward Shift: Cost↑, Higher taxes, Natural calamity, Fewer firms

Elasticity of Demand & Supply

Elasticity measures the degree of responsiveness of quantity to a change in a determinant.
Ed = (ΔQ / Q) ÷ (ΔP / P) = (ΔQ × P) / (ΔP × Q)

5 Types of Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

TypeEd ValueCurve ShapeExample
Perfectly ElasticEd = ∞Horizontal lineImaginary / ideal
Perfectly InelasticEd = 0Vertical lineLife-saving medicines
Unitary ElasticEd = 1Rectangular hyperbolaProportionate change
Elastic (More)Ed > 1Flatter curveLuxury goods
Inelastic (Less)Ed < 1Steeper curveRice, wheat, salt, petrol
Memory Aid: "Perfect Elastic → ∞ (Hotel), Perfect Inelastic → 0 (Medicine), Unitary → 1 (Hyperbola), >1 Luxury, <1 Necessity"

Income Elasticity

Responsiveness of demand to change in consumer income.

Normal goods → Positive
Inferior goods → Negative
Necessities → Low positive

Cross Elasticity

Responsiveness of demand for one good to change in price of another.

Substitutes → Positive (tea & coffee)
Complements → Negative (car & petrol)

📏 Elasticity of Supply

Es = (ΔQ / Q) ÷ (ΔP / P)
TypeEs ValueCurveExample
Perfectly InelasticEs = 0VerticalFresh fish — must sell today
Perfectly ElasticEs = ∞HorizontalIdeal / theoretical
UnitaryEs = 1Line through originProportionate change
Relatively ElasticEs > 1FlatterElectronics, furniture
Relatively InelasticEs < 1SteeperAgriculture, steel plants

🔢 Solved Examples

Example 1 — Mobile Company P₁=₹4500, Q₁=10,000 | P₂=₹4000, Q₂=12,000
ΔQ=2000, ΔP=−500
Ed = (2000/10000) ÷ (500/4500) = 0.2 ÷ 0.111 = 1.8 → Elastic demand
Example 2 — Demand Function D = 10 − 2P, find Ed at P=4 dQ/dP = −2, At P=4: Q = 10−8 = 2
Ed = |−2| × (4/2) = 4 → Highly elastic
Example 3 — P = 500 − 0.1Q, sell Q=4500 P = 500 − 0.1(4500) = ₹50
dQ/dP = −10
Ed = 10 × (50/4500) ≈ 0.11 → Inelastic demand

Market Equilibrium

Equilibrium = market situation where Quantity Demanded = Quantity Supplied. No excess demand or surplus. Market is in balance.
Equilibrium → Qd = Qs → No shortage, No surplus

⬆️ Price Above Equilibrium

Supply > Demand → Surplus
Sellers reduce price → moves toward equilibrium

⬇️ Price Below Equilibrium

Demand > Supply → Shortage
Sellers raise price → moves toward equilibrium

🔄 Effects of Demand/Supply Changes on Equilibrium

ChangePrice EffectQuantity Effect
Demand increases (right shift)↑ Rises↑ Rises
Demand decreases (left shift)↓ Falls↓ Falls
Supply increases (right shift)↓ Falls↑ Rises
Supply decreases (left shift)↑ Rises↓ Falls
Both D & S increase equallyNo change↑ Rises
Both D & S decrease equallyNo change↓ Falls
Solved Example D = 50−2P | S = 20+3P
Equilibrium: 50−2P = 20+3P → 5P = 30 → P* = ₹6
Q* = 50−12 = 38 units
Excess demand at P=3: D=44, S=29 → Excess Demand = 15 units

Production Function & Law of Variable Proportion

Production Function shows technical relationship between inputs and output.
Q = f(La, L, K, O)

Short Run (Variable Proportion)

Capital/land = FIXED. Labour = VARIABLE. Output changed by varying labour only.

Long Run (Fixed Proportion)

ALL factors variable. All inputs increased in same proportion. Factor ratio stays constant.

📊 TP, AP, MP

ConceptFormulaExample
Total Product (TP)Sum of all output5 workers produce 100 units → TP=100
Average Product (AP)AP = TP / L100/5 = 20 units per worker
Marginal Product (MP)MP = ΔTP / ΔL4 workers→80, 5 workers→100 → MP=(100−80)/1=20
AP-MP Relationship: MP>AP → AP rises | MP<AP → AP falls | MP=AP → AP is MAXIMUM

📉 Law of Variable Proportion — 3 Stages

Statement: When variable factor units are increased with fixed factor, TP first rises at increasing rate, then at diminishing rate, then decreases.

Stage I — IRF (Increasing Returns)

✅ RATIONAL STAGE
MP rises, reaches max at point of inflexion
AP rises, TP rises fast
Fixed factors underutilised
Division of labour benefits

Stage II — DRF (Diminishing Returns)

✅ RATIONAL STAGE
MP falls but stays positive
AP falls, TP rises slowly
MP=AP at start of Stage II
Ends when MP=0, TP=Maximum

Stage III — NRF (Negative Returns)

❌ IRRATIONAL STAGE
MP becomes negative
TP declines
Too many workers, overcrowding
No firm operates here

Assumptions: One variable factor (labour) | Technology constant | Homogeneous units | Short run
09 📈

Economies of Scale

Economies of Scale = Cost advantages when scale of production increases. More production → Lower cost per unit.

🏢 Internal Economies

Arise within the firm when it expands. Benefits from its own growth.

  • Labour Economies
  • Technical Economies
  • Managerial Economies
  • Financial Economies
  • Marketing Economies
  • Risk-Bearing Economies

🌐 External Economies

Arise due to growth of the entire industry, not the firm alone.

  • Economies of Localisation
  • Economies of Information
  • Economies of Vertical Disintegration
  • Economies of By-product

🔍 Internal Economies — Detailed

FinancialEasy loans at lower interest, credit purchasesBig firms get bank loans at cheaper rates

Cobb-Douglas Production Function

Definition A mathematical model showing the relationship between output and inputs (Labour & Capital).
Q = A · Lα · Kβ

🔑 Variables Explained

SymbolMeaningRange
QTotal Output—
LLabour Input—
KCapital Input—
ATechnology/Efficiency Parameter—
α (alpha)Output elasticity of Labour — % change in Q when L↑1% (K fixed)0 to 1
β (beta)Output elasticity of Capital — % change in Q when K↑1% (L fixed)0 to 1

📐 Returns to Scale (α + β)

α + β = 1

Constant Returns to Scale — Output ↑ by same % as inputs

α + β > 1

Increasing Returns to Scale — Output ↑ by MORE % than inputs

α + β < 1

Decreasing Returns to Scale — Output ↑ by LESS % than inputs

🔑 Key Features

1. Diminishing Returns

Increasing one input (other constant) → marginal product eventually declines.

2. Constant Elasticity of Substitution

Labour & capital can substitute each other at a constant rate.

3. Positive Marginal Products

Both L and K have positive but diminishing marginal productivity.

4. Income Share

α = Labour's share of income
β = Capital's share of income

🔢 Solved Examples

Derive MP of Labour: Q = ALαKβ
MPL = ∂Q/∂L = αALα−1Kβ = α(Q/L)
Q = 2K½L½, K=16, L=36
a) Q = 2×4×6 = 48 units
b) MPL = ∂Q/∂L = 2K½ × ½L−½ = K½/L½ = 4/6 = 0.667
c) APK = Q/K = 48/16 = 3
d) Q=40, L=25: 40 = 2K½×5 → K½=4 → K=16
Q = 3L½K½, Labour increases by 10% — find % change in output
α = ½ → % change in Q = α × % change in L = 0.5 × 10% = 5% increase
